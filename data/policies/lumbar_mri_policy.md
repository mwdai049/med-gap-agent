# Lumbar Spine MRI Medical Necessity Policy

## Pathway 1: Persistent symptoms after conservative therapy

Lumbar spine MRI may be supported when low back pain or radicular symptoms persist for at least 6 weeks.
Conservative treatment should be attempted first, such as physical therapy, medication, or activity modification.
Symptoms should not have meaningfully improved with conservative treatment.
Imaging should be expected to affect management, such as injection planning, surgical planning, or specialist escalation.

## Pathway 2: Red flag or urgent neurologic concern

Lumbar spine MRI may be supported when a new or progressive neurologic deficit is documented.
Relevant findings can include focal weakness, reflex asymmetry, or exam findings supporting radiculopathy.
Red flag symptoms may also support advanced imaging, including bowel or bladder dysfunction, saddle sensory disturbance, infection concern, malignancy concern, or major trauma.

## Documentation gaps

Duration of symptoms, treatments tried, response to conservative care, exam findings, and expected impact on management should be documented when available.
If required evidence is missing and no pathway is met, the case should be routed for additional documentation rather than treated as a clear approval.
When the documentation does not explain how MRI findings would change treatment, that gap should be surfaced explicitly.

## Exclusions

Persistent pain alone is less persuasive when there is no documented conservative treatment trial, no objective neurologic finding, and no explanation of how MRI would change management.
The absence of documentation should support a request for additional information rather than overconfident approval.

## Definitions

Conservative therapy may include physical therapy, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory medications, other medication trials, home exercise, or activity modification.
Radicular symptoms include pain radiating from the low back into the leg in a pattern suggesting nerve root irritation.

## Emergency red flags

Low back or radicular pain with new urinary retention, bowel dysfunction, saddle anesthesia, or bilateral leg weakness may indicate cauda equina syndrome.
These red flag presentations should be escalated for immediate clinical review.
Routine conservative therapy or duration requirements may not apply when urgent neurologic compromise is suspected.

## General imaging principles

Advanced imaging is stronger when the request is tied to a downstream management decision.
Examples include surgical planning, injection planning, specialist referral, or evaluation of a documented neurologic deficit.
Persistent pain alone is usually less persuasive than persistent pain plus objective findings or a management-changing plan.
